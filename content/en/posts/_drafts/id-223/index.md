# Introduction

You've done the leg work and applied for dozens, if not hundreds of jobs, and you check your email one day to an email requesting an interview. GREAT! you think to yourself, as your heart rate rises, your excitement bubbling over so much your hands are literally quivering. Once the excitement dies down, the mist from cloud 9 starts to recede, and the reality sets in: now you actually need to pass the interview. So what now?

# Preparing for Your Interview

This is a step that a lot of people either just straight up don't do, or do poorly. Even people who have done their fair share of interviews still for some reason underestimate the importance of this step, but I can tell you from personal experience, talking with a wide array of hiring managers, and having had to hire a few people myself, that this might actually be the most important part of your job search. The difference between a candidate that has prepared well for an interview and one who tries to "wing it" is painfully obvious to the hiring team. While it may not seem like a huge deal, little details like being intimately familiar with the role, the company you applied for, and the industry they operate in, can make all of the difference in the world. If there are two candidates that are in the same ballpark skill-wise, but one of them showed a high level of preparedness by knowing details about the company, a majority of the time the candidate with more company knowledge is going to win out.

## Company Research

One of the first things that you should do is check out the company website. This is rarely going to give you much insight into your actual role, but what you can typically get are things like:

- The company mission statement

- A list of values the company encourages

- Recent news

- How many open roles they have

- What, and how many, departments exist

You might also consider checking if the company has any social media accounts, and give them a quick review. You can get a feel for the pulse of general happenings, and community engagement, as well as glean some insight into public interactions with their social media profiles. Twitter, Facebook, and TikTok tend to be decent sources for this sort of social presence research and can be helpful.

### Diving Deeper - Alternative Information Sources

You are definitely going to want to get more information than just what the company is willing to offer publically. In order to really ask proper questions when it's your turn, you're going to want to have a little bit more information at your disposal, so where can you get it? One of the best ways is to just simply google the company and skim interesting-looking articles involving them. Things that you might be looking for are any major objectives the company is pursing, projects they might be working on, recently awarded contracts, and new markets.  
  
If you find out the company you want to interview with has recently entered a new market segment, this is definitely something you are going to want to ask about in the interview: this not only shows your interest in the company's movements but will give you an opportunity to ask questions about how this new push is being handled and spot any early red flags that you will want to have answers for before making a decision on accepting the role. If the role you are pursuing is part of a new company unit, for instance, the culture can be very fluid, and likely is going to function differently than well established units. This would be a perfect opportunity for you to ask about how the unit is coming together, whether it is being filled with outside candidates or internal transfers, and what the leadership of the unit is going to consist of. All of these things can be important for different reasons depending on what stage of your career you are at.  
  
If you are looking to quickly make a mark, joining a new company unit can be a real boon for your career, because new units are inherently going to get a lot of attention from decision-makers so they can determine if the unit is going to be long-term tenable for the company. Making an impact in such a unit can draw the right kind of attention, and could be a great opportunity to quickly move into a higher position. On the other hand, if you are a little newer in your career, or recently moved up a level, this might not be ideal, as there might not be as established a training program, leadership could be newer to the company, and certain aspects of success in the unit might be out of your control completely. In cases like this, you might actually want to be trying to get into one of the more established units in order to take advantage of proven processes, documentation, and mentorship opportunities, as well as typically much more clear and defined expectations of success. Regardless, this is information that will be useful to you during your interview.

### Culture Research

Another important point of research is to get a finger on the pulse of the company culture and working environment. While you can always just ask what the company culture is like at the company, this is not typically the best way to get honest and open feedback. So how else can you find this information? One of the most effective ways is to talk to the actual employees, both current and past. There are both passive and active ways to go about researching employee opinions.

### Employee Research - Passive

Passive research can involve things such as checking review sites such as glassdoor, indeed, and other similar sites. For well-established companies, there are typically going to be a large number of reviews, which will give you a pretty good idea of the general sentiment of working there. Just like all reviews, you're going to likely find a wide range, so you just have to use your best judgment and look for any patterns both positive and negative. Do a lot of reviews contradict each other? If they are spread out over time, this could be an indicator of a changing environment, so you'll want to make sure the trend is in a positive direction. If a lot of contradictory reviews are from the same time period, that could be a huge red flag that the company is incentivizing current employees to leave reviews in order to counter-act a negative trend. If you see this, it's something you most certainly want to get answers for during your interview, and be especially mindful of any non-answers or attempts to brush away your concerns.
